
# Design implications – the foundations of a new service

The conditions in 'What good looks like' describe what must be true.

The design implications set out what the service must do differently to address the causes of quality issues and delay uncovered in the discovery.

## The service provides a defined workflow for the case

The service must provide a clear workflow for how the case progresses from the start to first hearing.

This workflow is based on the state of the case, not a sequence of tasks.

- the workflow reflects how the case develops, rather than individual activities
- progression is based on the case reaching defined states
- the case is worked through end-to-end

This replaces the current reliance on task-based working, where cases are progressed through disconnected steps and deadlines rather than understood as a whole.

## The service prevents cases progressing when they are not fit to proceed

The service needs to stop the case moving forward until it is in a state that can properly support the next stage.

This is not only about whether information is missing. It is also about whether the case is good enough to be acted on.

- Important information needed to understand the case is there.
- Missing material has been identified and acted on.
- Obvious errors and contradictions have been addressed.
- The case can be understood and used.
- The charge and available evidence are in a state that can support legal consideration or service.

This stops cases reaching later stages with missing key evidence, the wrong charges, or other basic problems still unresolved.

## The service captures and retains the prosecution case

The prosecution case is explicitly captured in a way that meets the lawyer's mental model and persists through the life of the case.

It is not inferred from documents or reconstructed at each stage. Instead, it is recorded in a structured way that can be reused and shared.

- The charge, evidence relied upon and key facts are captured once and updated if the case changes.
- The information is linked directly to the underlying material.
- The prosecution case captured can be used across service, and to aid disclosure and advocacy.

Capturing the prosecution case in this way enables the system to generate outputs directly from it. This replaces the need to assemble and manage bundles, where the case is reconstructed into documents for different stages. It could also pre-populate PET forms and support continuous disclosure.

The case is defined and then used throughout the journey.

## The service supports legal quality and procedural compliance

The service must support legal decisions being made, recorded and used in a way that meets established standards.

Decisions are grounded in the available evidence, and the reasons for those decisions are captured and visible. The prosecution case can be clearly understood and checked.

The service supports compliance with procedural requirements, including the timely service of the Initial Details of the Prosecution Case.

- material can be served as soon as practicable
- the case is complete enough to meet its purpose at that stage
- required steps are supported as part of the case

This ensures that case quality is supported consistently, rather than depending on individual practice.

## The service produces reliable data and a clear audit trail

The case is captured in a way that produces accurate and usable data.

Charges, evidence, decisions and case progression are structured and recorded consistently, so data reflects what has actually happened.

A clear audit trail is maintained as part of the case.

- decisions and actions are recorded
- changes can be seen and understood over time
- it is clear what has happened, when, and why

This supports accountability, supervision and continuous improvement.

## The service treats evidence as part of the case

Evidence is handled as part of the case, not separately. It is:

- Available as first class items
- Linked directly to the case
- Carried through all stages

This ensures evidence is consistently available and usable when needed, rather than being buried in documents, scattered across systems, or treated as an attachment to the case rather than part of it.

## The service maintains a complete view of the case

The full state of the case is visible at any point in time.

This includes:

- What has been received
- What is missing
- What is in progress
- What has been shared, with who

The case does not depend on individuals holding this information separately, and can be understood as a whole.

## The service ensures work and decisions persist

Information, decisions and understanding persist across stages.

The case does not need to be rechecked, reworked or reinterpreted from scratch as it moves forward.

This reduces repeated effort and supports consistency between review, service, advocacy and later case progression.

## The service uses automation to make the case usable

The service must take on work that is currently done manually to prepare the case.

This includes organising and structuring material, linking evidence to the case, and generating outputs from the case.

This removes manual "tidying" work, where information is reformatted, reorganised, checked or moved between systems.

Effort is focused on progressing and understanding the case, rather than preparing it to be usable.

## The service uses AI to support understanding and preparation

AI is used to support how the case is understood and prepared.

- summaries and key information are generated from the material
- relationships within the case are surfaced
- draft outputs support review

This reduces the effort needed to prepare cases, particularly at high volume.

AI supports users but does not determine the prosecution case or legal decisions.

## The service supports the prioritisation of cases

The service supports different levels of attention based on the needs of the case.

Effort is directed by importance, risk and CPS priorities.

- High-volume cases can be prepared in advance and validated.
- Cases needing greater scrutiny have more detailed legal input.

All cases have legal input, but the level and timing of that input reflects the nature and importance of the case.

## The service enables informed interaction between people and organisations

Work on the case is based on a shared understanding across all those involved.

This includes:

- CPS colleagues working in different roles
- police and CPS
- advocates and reviewing lawyers

Requests for material or action are specific, contextual and based on what is needed and why. The state of the case is visible to those involved.

This supports effective working relationships and reduces repeated chasing, miscommunication and reliance on informal workarounds.

Interaction is based on a shared understanding of the case, rather than assumptions or partial views.

## The service provides direct access to the case and evidence

Users can access the case and the evidence it relies on, including defendants.

The case is presented in an accessible format and does not rely on PDF bundles.

Evidence can be accessed directly, rather than through references in documents or separate systems.

This ensures the case can be seen and understood by all users.

## The service supports preparation before the hearing

The case is available early enough to be used.

This supports preparation in line with legal expectations on timely service.

- Work is completed before the hearing, within normal working hours.
- The case can be understood and acted on before court.
- Advocates, defence practitioners and defendants can prepare in advance.

The hearing is used for decision-making, not preparation.
