# Show and tell 5: Helping reviewing lawyers review a case and share it with the relevant parties as early as possible

## The problem we're trying to solve

At the moment, case materials exist as a pile of documents, mostly PDF. 

There's nothing to connect a document to what it actually proves.

As a lawyer looks at the material, they're:

- deciding what the charge is and whether the material supports it
- spotting what needs redacting
- noticing what's missing

But the system doesn't capture that legal reasoning when it happens. 

As a result:

- Lawyers copy content out of CMS into personal tools to read and annotate it, then upload those notes back as more documents in the pile
- Review summaries and case notes are buried and hard to find; they may be low quality, out of date, and never read by the next person on the case
- When evidence changes — for example when a witness withdraws — there's no way to see the knock-on effect without re-reading the whole case from scratch
- The same analysis is repeated at multiple stages — initial review, IDPC, PTPH form, opening at trial — because the lawyer’s analysis isn't stored, certainly not in any usable form. The lawyer ends up making the same judgement more than once, with the risk that the second decision doesn't quite match the first
- Getting a case from arrival to shared involves a long chain of people and manually raised tasks: a casework assistant opens and tidies the case, raises a task for a reviewing lawyer, the lawyer eventually reviews it, then raises more tasks to prepare a bundle, warn a witness, and whatever else is needed
- Lawyers spend time on admin such as writing statements confirming they've complied with charging rules, rather than reviewing cases

And for users downstream:

- Reviews are either not done or done badly, and information, evidence or elements aren't captured. The advocate can't do their job properly, so they start early on the day of the hearing to get a grip on the case — effectively doing the review from scratch, on top of everything else they're there to do
- Defendants don't receive the case ahead of the first hearing, which doesn't comply with the rules. It also matters practically: if the prosecution case is clear to the defence early, they won't fight it, and the defendant will plead guilty sooner — fewer hearings, shorter hearings, fewer adjournments

All of this is long-winded, error prone and wasteful, with a significant operational cost. 

## Hypothesis 1

If lawyers can read, highlight and annotate case materials within the system — and those annotations connect directly to structured data like charges and evidence — then:

- Analysis is captured once and reused across the case journey, rather than repeated from scratch at every stage
- The lawyer or advocate can understand the case quickly from structured annotations, rather than hunting for a buried summary document or re-reading everything
- When evidence changes, the impact is immediately visible — showing which charges are affected without re-reading the case
- Lawyers no longer need to leave the system to do their actual work

## Hypothesis 2

If finishing a review itself triggers what happens next — the right notifications, bundles and sharing to the right people, with assembly and quality/redaction checks automated where that doesn't erode trust or remove necessary judgement calls — then:

- Cases get shared without a chain of manually raised tasks, reducing the admin burden on reviewing lawyers and other users
- The quality of what gets shared is consistent and much easier to understand
- Defendants can access their own case, and are updated whenever CPS updates it
